MATERNAL HEALTH AND BLACK WOMEN — HEALTH EDUCATION GUIDE

The Crisis
Black women in the United States are 2 to 3 times more likely to die from pregnancy-related causes than white women. This disparity exists across all income and education levels — meaning even Black women with advanced degrees and high incomes face significantly higher maternal mortality risk. This is one of the most severe racial health disparities in America.

Common Pregnancy-Related Complications
Black women face higher rates of:
- Preeclampsia — dangerous high blood pressure during pregnancy
- Eclampsia — seizures caused by severe preeclampsia
- Postpartum hemorrhage — excessive bleeding after birth
- Blood clots
- Cardiomyopathy — heart muscle disease related to pregnancy
- Severe infections

Why the Disparity Exists
The higher maternal mortality rate among Black women is not explained by biology. It is driven by:

Systemic Racism in Healthcare — Studies show Black women's pain is frequently underestimated and undertreated by medical providers. Symptoms are more likely to be dismissed or delayed in diagnosis.

Chronic Stress — The ongoing stress of experiencing racism, known as weathering, has measurable physical effects on the body over time including higher blood pressure and weakened immune response.

Limited Access to Care — Black women are more likely to receive care at under-resourced hospitals and less likely to have access to high-quality prenatal care.

Implicit Bias — Healthcare providers may hold unconscious biases that affect the quality of care Black patients receive.

Prenatal Care
Early and consistent prenatal care significantly reduces risk. Key steps include:
- Begin prenatal care as soon as you know you are pregnant
- Attend all scheduled prenatal appointments
- Monitor blood pressure regularly throughout pregnancy
- Communicate all symptoms to your provider even if you feel dismissed
- Ask about your risk factors specifically

Warning Signs During and After Pregnancy
Seek immediate medical attention if you experience:
- Severe headache that does not go away
- Vision changes including blurring or seeing spots
- Sudden swelling of face, hands, or feet
- Chest pain or difficulty breathing
- Excessive bleeding
- High fever
- Thoughts of harming yourself or your baby

Postpartum Mental Health
Up to 1 in 5 women experience postpartum depression. Black women are less likely to be screened and less likely to receive treatment. Symptoms include persistent sadness, difficulty bonding with baby, extreme fatigue, anxiety, and feeling like a bad mother. These are medical symptoms — not personal failures.

Advocating For Yourself
- Bring a support person to appointments who can speak up for you
- Write down your symptoms and questions before appointments
- If you say you are in pain, insist on being taken seriously
- Know that you have the right to a second opinion or a different provider
- If something feels wrong after birth, go to the emergency room — do not wait

Important Disclaimer
This information is for educational purposes only and is based on medically reviewed sources including the CDC, NIH, and Office on Women's Health. It is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Always consult your healthcare provider for diagnosis and treatment.
