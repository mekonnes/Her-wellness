NUTRITION AND BLACK WOMEN — HEALTH EDUCATION GUIDE

The Reality
Nutrition plays a critical role in preventing and managing the health conditions that disproportionately affect Black women including hypertension, fibroids, diabetes, and heart disease. However, accessing healthy food, affording nutritious options, and navigating conflicting nutrition information can all be challenging. This guide provides practical, culturally relevant nutrition information grounded in medical research.

Why Nutrition Matters Specifically for Black Women
Black women face higher rates of:
- Type 2 diabetes — diet is a primary risk factor
- Hypertension — strongly influenced by sodium intake
- Obesity — linked to chronic disease risk
- Fibroids — some research suggests diet may influence fibroid growth
- Heart disease — the leading cause of death for Black women

Cultural Food and Identity
Soul food and African cuisine are deeply connected to Black cultural identity and community. This guide does not suggest abandoning cultural foods. Instead it focuses on understanding how to enjoy cultural foods in ways that support long-term health while honoring tradition.

Key Nutritional Principles

Reduce Sodium
Black women tend to be more salt sensitive meaning sodium has a stronger effect on blood pressure. The American Heart Association recommends no more than 2,300 mg of sodium per day ideally closer to 1,500 mg.
- Cook with herbs and spices instead of salt
- Choose low sodium versions of canned goods
- Rinse canned beans and vegetables to reduce sodium
- Limit processed and packaged foods which are high in hidden sodium

Increase Fruits and Vegetables
Aim for at least 5 servings of fruits and vegetables per day. These provide fiber, potassium, and antioxidants that protect against chronic disease.
- Dark leafy greens like collards, kale, and spinach are nutrient-dense and culturally familiar
- Sweet potatoes are high in potassium and fiber
- Okra, black-eyed peas, and other traditional vegetables are nutritious choices

Choose Whole Grains
Replace refined grains with whole grains where possible:
- Brown rice instead of white rice
- Whole wheat bread instead of white bread
- Oats, quinoa, and farro are nutritious options

Prioritize Lean Protein
- Fish especially fatty fish like salmon, mackerel, and sardines are high in omega-3 fatty acids which reduce inflammation and support heart health
- Legumes including beans, lentils, and peas are high in protein and fiber and are affordable
- Skinless poultry and lean cuts of meat
- Limit processed meats like sausage and bacon which are high in sodium and saturated fat

Healthy Fats
- Use olive oil or avocado oil instead of butter or lard when possible
- Avocados, nuts, and seeds provide healthy unsaturated fats
- Limit fried foods which are high in saturated and trans fats

Limit Added Sugar
High sugar intake contributes to diabetes, obesity, and inflammation.
- Limit sugary drinks including soda, juice, and sweetened tea
- Choose water, unsweetened tea, or sparkling water
- Read nutrition labels — sugar is often hidden in sauces, condiments, and packaged foods

Managing Specific Conditions Through Diet

For Hypertension — Follow the DASH diet. Reduce sodium, increase potassium through bananas, sweet potatoes, and leafy greens, and limit alcohol.

For Fibroids — Some research suggests reducing red meat, increasing green vegetables, and limiting alcohol may help manage fibroid symptoms. More research is needed.

For Diabetes Prevention and Management — Focus on low glycemic foods, portion control, limiting refined carbohydrates and sugar, and eating regular meals to stabilize blood sugar.

For Heart Health — Increase omega-3 fatty acids, reduce saturated fat, increase fiber, and maintain a healthy weight.

Food Access and Affordability
Healthy eating does not require expensive specialty foods. Practical tips:
- Dried and canned beans are affordable, nutritious, and versatile
- Frozen vegetables are just as nutritious as fresh and are more affordable
- Buy seasonal produce which is cheaper and fresher
- Community gardens, farmers markets, and food cooperatives may offer affordable fresh produce
- SNAP benefits can be used at many farmers markets

Important Disclaimer
This information is for educational purposes only and is based on medically reviewed sources. It is not a substitute for advice from a registered dietitian or healthcare provider. If you have a specific health condition please consult your doctor or dietitian before making significant dietary changes.
