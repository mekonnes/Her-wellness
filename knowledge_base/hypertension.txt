HYPERTENSION AND BLACK WOMEN — HEALTH EDUCATION GUIDE

The Reality
Black women have some of the highest rates of high blood pressure (hypertension) in the world. Nearly 57% of Black women in the United States have hypertension — significantly higher than white women. Black women also develop high blood pressure earlier in life and are more likely to experience severe complications including stroke, heart disease, and kidney failure.

What Is Hypertension
Blood pressure is the force of blood pushing against the walls of your arteries. Hypertension is when that force is consistently too high. It is measured in two numbers:
- Systolic pressure — the top number, pressure when your heart beats
- Diastolic pressure — the bottom number, pressure when your heart rests between beats

Blood Pressure Categories:
- Normal: below 120/80
- Elevated: 120-129 / below 80
- High Blood Pressure Stage 1: 130-139 / 80-89
- High Blood Pressure Stage 2: 140 or higher / 90 or higher
- Hypertensive Crisis: higher than 180 / higher than 120 — seek emergency care immediately

Why Black Women Are Disproportionately Affected
The higher rates of hypertension in Black women are not explained by genetics alone. Contributing factors include:

Chronic Stress from Racism — The constant psychological stress of experiencing racism and discrimination raises blood pressure over time. This is a well-documented medical phenomenon known as weathering.

Limited Access to Care — Black women are less likely to have regular access to preventive healthcare where hypertension would be detected and managed early.

Implicit Bias — Black women's symptoms are more likely to be dismissed or undertreated by healthcare providers.

Socioeconomic Factors — Stress from financial insecurity, food access, and neighborhood safety all contribute to elevated blood pressure.

Salt Sensitivity — Research suggests Black Americans may be more sensitive to dietary sodium, though this does not explain the full disparity.

Symptoms
Hypertension is often called the silent killer because most people have no symptoms. When symptoms do occur they may include:
- Severe headache
- Fatigue or confusion
- Vision problems
- Chest pain
- Difficulty breathing
- Irregular heartbeat
- Blood in urine
- Pounding in chest, neck, or ears

If you experience these symptoms seek medical attention immediately.

Complications of Untreated Hypertension
- Heart attack
- Stroke — Black women have significantly higher stroke rates
- Heart failure
- Kidney disease
- Vision loss
- Sexual dysfunction
- Peripheral artery disease

Prevention and Management
Lifestyle changes can significantly lower blood pressure:

Diet — Follow a low sodium diet. The DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) is specifically designed to lower blood pressure and emphasizes fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean protein, and low-fat dairy.

Exercise — Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week such as walking, swimming, or cycling.

Weight Management — Losing even a small amount of weight can significantly reduce blood pressure.

Limit Alcohol — No more than one drink per day for women.

Quit Smoking — Smoking raises blood pressure and damages blood vessels.

Stress Management — Chronic stress raises blood pressure. Practices like meditation, deep breathing, yoga, and adequate sleep help.

Medication — Many people need medication in addition to lifestyle changes. Common medications include ACE inhibitors, beta blockers, calcium channel blockers, and diuretics. Take all medications as prescribed.

Monitor At Home — Home blood pressure monitors are affordable and allow you to track your blood pressure between doctor visits.

Advocating For Yourself
- Know your numbers — get your blood pressure checked regularly
- If your blood pressure is high ask your doctor about a treatment plan
- Do not stop taking medication without consulting your doctor
- If your concerns are dismissed seek a second opinion
